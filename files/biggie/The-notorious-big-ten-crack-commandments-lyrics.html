About Ten Crack Commandments

Biggie presents himself as the Moses of the drug game, delivering his commandments from on high to young dealers. The general advice given is to trust no one (even family and friends), watch your back, and not try any risky financial maneuvers — inexperienced dealers, and banks, fail because they often don’t heed this advice

In referencing the Ten Commandments, Biggie plays on the struggle in the ghetto between the force of God and the dark forces of capitalism and human nature, much like Jay-Z’s “D'Evils” Ultimately, as in “D'Evils”, the ruthless street religion is stronger than Christianity

Rocmon breaks down Biggies inspiration for his infamous Ten Crack Commandments track

Great video — dude flips to a page in The Source (back when it had respect) and shows the inspiration

“Ten! Ten crack commandments! Ah-ah-ah..”

In a slightly sociological look at the Reagan Era, this is direct result of the Chambers Brothers. (The Chambers Brothers ran over 300 crackhouses at the height of their power in Detroit). When the police would raid various Chambers houses, they would find various commandments for their employees which drew comparisons to Fortune 500 companies such as Chrysler (not to mention their annual income is alleged to top Chrysler’s for the same years). The Chambers Brothers are widely responsible for rope chains as well.
