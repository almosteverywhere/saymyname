The Most Heart-wrenching and Heartwarming Moments from Ariana Grande's 'Yours Truly'

By:
tatyahna

834

Editor

Ariana Grande’s debut Yours Truly dropped to generally positive reviews. While she started her career in musical theater and later through tween Nickelodeon shows like Victorious and Sam & Cat, she’s breaking through into music with full force. Ariana says the album is about:

the most important years of my life have been captured in this album and some of my most important experiences like relationship-wise. So many things I’ve learned I’ll remember when I listen back to these songs. It’s just such a special thing for me. I can’t wait for my fans to hear it. -Ariana Grande to MTV Sept 2013

That being said, it’s full of love and heartache, so here are the best examples of each.

http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=32rVxVXdZ58 Honeymoon Avenue
This entire song is one big piece of heartbreak, and while it was originally recorded much more upbeat in 2011, the new slowed down version becomes much more personal after Ariana’s recent split with boyfriend Jai Brooks. With lines like “I feel like my heart is stuck
in bumper to bumper traffic, I’m going to crash ‘Cause I can’t have you the way that I want” you would tear up too.

Big Sean’s Verse in “Right There”

Unlike Mac Miller’s verse in “The Way”, Big Sean shows some seriously swoon worthy insecurities in this track he’s like: “Would you catch me before the pavement if my Benz turned to public transportation” but obviously he doesn’t know what a ride or die Ariana is.

This collab is even sweeter when you know how much Ariana idolizes Sean, she’s known to throw down a verse of Guap every so often.

Better Left Unsaid
This is one of the most dance-y songs on the album, but the overall story is pretty depressing. It’s basically a post-breakup anthem, where Ariana misses her past love interest after saying some things she now regrets. She’s not going to mope in heartbreak though, she’s going to go out and kiss that dude without talking about the past — because somethings are better left unsaid. Tbh, nobody would complain about Ariana crawling back for a late night reunion.

Baby I
Ariana pays homage to a pop princess inspiration with very Oops I Did It Again “yeah yeah yeahs” but this song is so much more than that. The hook is so heart melting “All I’m tryna say is you’re my everything baby but every time I try to say it words, they only complicate it” explaining how butterflies get in the way of actual verbal expression. Ariana tackles this by buying her boyfriends sloths instead.

You’ll Never Know
Ariana slams down a dude who waited too long to let her know how he feels. She has no time for games, she’s out here sounding seriously Mimi, so “Don’t get mad at her cause you’ve been replaced, know your place” Ariana doesn’t care about breaking hearts, ain’t nobody got time for relationship saboteurs.

Tatooed Heart
Tattooed Heart easily could have been something that played during any ‘50s prom movie. Sugary sweet proclamations of love like, “I wanna say we’re going steady, like it’s 1954” prove all she wants is a cute simple relationship where her dude loves her eternally and internally. Ariana also has a literal tattooed heart on her toe!

She’s Just Being Miley... and Every Other Pop Star in American Music History Ever

By:
SanFranG

177

Editor
Moderator

A Rap Genius University Perspective on that Infamous VMAs Performance

Dr. Sarah Lappas

As a popular music scholar specializing in the history of African American music, the last few days have quite frankly been thrilling for me. Ever since Miley Cyrus performed at the VMAs, it feels like all of a sudden everybody’s talking about all of the things I think and write and teach about every day: how issues of race, class, and gender manifest themselves in contemporary popular music.

Now I’ve waited a few days for the dust to settle, and I’ve taken a lot of time to read conflicting perspectives about Miley’s performance. You’ve seen all the highlights I have: Miley’s performance was “really, really disturbing,” according to Mika Brzezinski, prompting a backlash of outrage against slut-shaming, and followed by critics who called her performance racist and compared it to blackface minstrelsy.

Normally when critics compare contemporary hip hop and pop acts to blackface minstrelsy I usually roll my eyes and dismiss it. It tends to be the go-to insult for critics who want to make a racially-charged case against a performer, and usually these critics make pretty facile comparisons that totally lack nuance. For example, T-Pain is not like a blackface minstrel just because he has a grill and makes crazy facial expressions. Eminem is not like a blackface minstrel just because he’s a white rapper.

In the case of Miley Cyrus’ performance at the VMAs, it’s actually a pretty appropriate comparison. As Eric Lott has so brilliantly explained in his beautiful book Love and Theft: Blackface Minstrelsy and the American Working Class, blackface minstrelsy was a genre that epitomized the contemporaneous “dialectical flickering” of racial envy and racial insult. It was a genre enjoyed and performed by black and white Americans alike. It was America’s very first national popular art form, as minstrel troupes criss-crossed the nation performing the same jokes, songs, skits, and dances. Before the advent of radio or television, America’s first national popular music was steeped in the grotesque racial stereotypes that mediated the anger, discomfort, and fear of a rapidly changing America: an America built on the backs of a commodified black underclass.

As the recording industry developed, the racial signifiers of American popular music were increasingly codified by a nation and industry obsessed with race. Race labels abounded (Victor, Swan, Paramount to name a few), signing blues, jazz, and gospel acts and marketed primarily to black audiences. Hillbilly records (yes, that was really what they were called) featured recordings of white folk singers from the Appalachians and provided the early roots for country music (still an overwhelmingly white genre). In the late 1940s race labels underwent a more politically correct makeover and were called rhythm and blues, or R&B: that moving target of a genre that nevertheless always basically means “black.”

While American popular music was born and developed in a society completely preoccupied with race (and has been marketed accordingly), it’s important to remember that being popular, cool, new, and edgy has most often meant being a musical act that American society found racially transgressive. Elvis’ career skyrocketed and he remains the legend he is today not only because he appropriated R&B songs from black artists (plenty of totally forgettable artists in the 50s and 60s did that), but because he infused them with a hillbilly style and the combination blew people’s minds. Michael Jackson became the King of Pop (and really invented what we think of as pop today—a kind of multicultural mainstream) by combining the “black and proud” howls, screams, and funk-laden riffs of the James Brown school with rock guitar riffs and the smooth synth sounds that sounded “white” to audiences in the 90s. Lil Wayne’s use of stylized Autotune on Knockout over a totally generic alt-rock guitar riff as he asks “Barbie” (Nicki Minaj) if she’s into black men is, from the perspective of an audience that really responds to aural and lyrical signifiers of race, totally brilliant. We are Americans and we, apparently, can’t get enough of that shit.

So Miley’s performance on Sunday, and the “transformation” she’s been undergoing over the past few months is akin to blackface minstrelsy to the extent that she’s clearly engaging in a kind of racial mimicry born of racial envy. But let’s be clear, that doesn’t make Miley’s performance particularly unique. She’s no Elvis and she sure as hell ain’t no MJ, but she is engaging in a long-established tradition of exploiting a combination of signifiers that audiences find racially transgressive in American popular music.

It’s painful because the historical and present-day realities of the subjugation of African American people is painful. People are angry because she objectified black women in her performance, and black women are too often objectified in the popular media and in real life. People are freaked out because she’s acting and sounding “black” in ways that make folks uncomfortable, and that’s also the reason that “We Can’t Stop” is topping the charts right now. People are pissed because the combination of Miley twerking and sticking her tongue out that far (how is that even possible?) is making their eyes bleed. As a nation, we are feeling all the feelings.

What I am happy about is that this performance is sparking a ton of meaningful discussions about how we represent and interpret race, class, and gender in popular music—signifiers of which are always being utilized and exploited in American pop music, but usually in much more insidious ways. The bottom line is that in a nation that was quite literally built on racial distinction and subjugation, there is no vocabulary (musical, visual, linguistic or otherwise) that isn’t implicated in that system of subjugation. When someone pushes the boundaries in a way that frightens or offends us, it’s more important than ever to look deeply at all of the real social issues that are informing our responses to a musical performance. And particularly on this 50th Anniversary of the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom, let’s remember that the movement MLK helped lead was just as much about alleviating financial inequality and the devastating lack of opportunities for meaningful economic engagement in predominantly black communities as it was about abolishing Jim Crow laws in the South. As American popular music often helps make so glaringly obvious, we still have a helluva a long way to go.
